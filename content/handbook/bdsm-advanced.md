If you have read our Beginner's Guide and spent time there — if you have negotiated, practised, used your safe words, mastered aftercare and built genuine trust with a partner — then this guide is for you. Advanced BDSM is not about escalating pain or elaborating equipment for its own sake. It is about going deeper: into longer, more layered scenes, more sophisticated techniques, and a more nuanced understanding of the psychological dimensions of power exchange.

The fundamentals do not change at this level. Consent, negotiation, safe words and aftercare remain as essential here as they were on day one. What changes is the complexity of what you can do with them.

## Scene Craft

A scene is a contained, negotiated BDSM encounter — a beginning, a middle and an end, with agreed parameters. At beginner level, scenes tend to be improvised and short. At advanced level, scene craft becomes its own discipline.

**Negotiation at this level** covers not just hard and soft limits but emotional triggers, physical restrictions (old injuries, medication, chronic conditions), the intended intensity arc of the scene, and what both partners want to feel and take away from it. Some experienced practitioners use written scene agreements for longer or more intense play.

**Scene structure:** A well-constructed scene typically builds through stages: grounding (connecting with your partner before play begins), build-up (increasing intensity and arousal gradually), peak (the most intense phase), wind-down (easing the intensity back), and aftercare. Experienced dominants learn to read their partner's physical and emotional state throughout and modulate accordingly — the ability to sense when to push and when to ease is a skill developed over time, not taught in a single session.

**Maintaining headspace:** Both dominant and submissive enter altered states during a scene — sometimes called "top space" and "sub space." Sub space is a dissociative, deeply relaxed state that some submissives enter under prolonged intense stimulation; it involves a flood of endorphins and can impair the ability to use a safe word effectively. An experienced dominant recognises the signs — glassy eyes, slurred speech, slowed response — and takes responsibility for safety even when their partner is no longer fully able to advocate for themselves.

## Impact Play: Going Further

Beginners start with hands. Advanced impact play introduces tools, each producing distinctly different sensations.

**Floggers** — leather tails on a handle — produce a "thuddy" sensation that spreads across a wide area. The sensation depends heavily on leather weight and tail length; a light suede flogger produces something close to a firm massage, while a heavy leather one produces significant impact. Flogging technique is a real skill: figure-eight swinging patterns, consistent rhythm and controlled placement take practice to develop. Target areas are the same as in basic spanking — the buttocks and upper thighs — plus the upper back and shoulders with more experience. Always avoid the spine, kidneys, tailbone and joints.

**Paddles** produce a concentrated, sharp impact. The sensation is more sting than thud. Start lighter than you think you need to; paddles are easier to over-use than most beginners expect. Warm up the area thoroughly beforehand.

**Crops** and **canes** produce precise, intense impact and are considered advanced tools. A crop can target very specific areas. A cane, at full application, produces the most intense impact sensation of any common implement and leaves marks. These require genuine practice — ideally on a pillow first — before use on a partner.

General principle across all impact play: start lighter than your target intensity, build gradually, check in regularly, and pay close attention to skin condition — redness, bruising and welting are expected; broken skin or deep bruising that doesn't emerge until the following day warrant stopping and reassessing.

## Rope Bondage

Standard wrist cuffs are beginner bondage. Rope work — particularly the Japanese art of Shibari — is something else entirely. It is technically demanding, aesthetically striking and produces a physical sensation (the pressure of rope against skin, the gentle restriction of movement) that purpose-made restraints cannot fully replicate. It also carries significant risk if done without proper knowledge.

**Before learning rope work:**

- Study anatomy, particularly the location of nerves most vulnerable to compression injury (the radial nerve at the inner elbow and wrist, the peroneal nerve at the outer knee, and the brachial plexus at the armpit are the most critical)
- Practice knots and ties before using them on a person
- Never perform suspension (lifting a partner off the ground with rope) without extensive in-person training from an experienced rigger
- Keep safety scissors accessible at all times
- Check in on sensation every few minutes during any tie — numbness, tingling, or colour change in extremities means release immediately

The reward, for those who invest in learning it properly, is considerable. Shibari in particular produces a meditative, deeply intimate experience for both partners that many describe as unlike anything else in their BDSM practice.

## Psychological Dominance

At an advanced level, the most effective dominance is often psychological rather than physical. Verbal direction, controlled pacing, the deliberate use of silence and waiting, enforced stillness without physical restraint — these tools are more demanding to wield skillfully than any physical implement, and they produce some of the most intense responses.

**Protocols and rituals** — agreed rules that the submissive follows within a scene or relationship — create a sustained psychological framework of dominance and submission that can extend beyond play sessions. This might include forms of address, required permissions, specific behaviours or dress codes. Their value is the continuity of the dynamic and the depth of trust and attention they require from both parties.

**Orgasm control and edging** — the dominant controls when and whether their partner is allowed to orgasm. This requires significant ongoing knowledge of your partner's responses and a high degree of attentiveness. Done well, it produces states of arousal that partners often describe as unlike anything in their previous experience.

## Sensory Deprivation: Advanced Applications

A beginner's blindfold evolves at advanced level into more complete sensory restriction. Sound-blocking earplugs or headphones removed of external sound leave a person with nothing but their own heartbeat and whatever touch their partner provides. The intensity of each sensation in this state is difficult to convey without experiencing it.

Hoods, specifically designed for BDSM use, provide both sensory restriction and a powerful psychological element — the experience of being "covered" shifts the headspace of many submissives significantly. Use only purpose-designed items with adequate ventilation, never improvise with materials that risk suffocation, and maintain verbal communication capability throughout.

## What to Avoid

No guide to advanced BDSM would be complete without a clear list of practices that require either specialist training or should be avoided entirely:

**Breath play** (any restriction of breathing, whether by hand, ligature or other means) is categorised as "edge play" — activities where risk of serious injury or death cannot be eliminated through care and preparation alone. No amount of experience makes it safe. It is responsible for a significant proportion of BDSM-related fatalities. The community consensus is broadly: don't.

**Electrical play** — if this interests you, use only purpose-designed, low-voltage devices (violet wands, TENS units adapted for this purpose). Never use mains electricity. Never apply electrical stimulation above the waist if your partner has a pacemaker or heart condition.

**Blood play** — carries infection risk and is firmly in the edge play category. Requires full STI knowledge of all partners and appropriate safety precautions.

## Continuing Education

Advanced BDSM is a practice that benefits enormously from community. Online communities, local munches (informal, non-play social gatherings within the kink community), and workshops run by experienced practitioners offer learning that no written guide can fully substitute for. The Kink community broadly operates on a foundation of education, peer support and ethical practice — engaging with it seriously will take your practice further than any amount of solo research.

The goal, at every level, remains the same: the exploration of trust, sensation and desire in ways that leave everyone involved feeling seen, safe and satisfied.
