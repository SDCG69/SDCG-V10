## What Is Shibari?

[image src="media/img/shibari-intro.jpg" alt="Shibari Rope Bondage"]

Shibari — from the Japanese *shibaru*, "to bind" — is the art of decorative rope bondage. It emerged from a centuries-old Japanese martial and penal tradition called *hojōjutsu*, the practice of restraining prisoners with rope according to strict, codified patterns. In the twentieth century it was adapted by artists, performers and erotic practitioners into the form recognised today: an intricate, deliberate and deeply intimate discipline that sits somewhere between art, meditation and physical practice.

In its modern form, shibari is as much about the experience between two people as it is about the rope itself. The person doing the tying — the *rigger* — and the person being tied — the *bunny* or *bottom* — enter a state of shared attention and trust that practitioners often describe as unlike anything else. The rope is the medium; the connection is the point.

You do not have to be an artist or an acrobat to begin. The foundational ties that most practitioners spend years returning to are simple enough to learn in an afternoon, deep enough to explore for a lifetime.

## Before You Begin: Safety First

Shibari carries real physical risks that do not apply to many other forms of intimate play. Understanding them before you touch a length of rope is not optional — it is the foundation the whole practice is built on.

### Nerve Damage

The most significant risk in rope bondage is radial nerve compression — a temporary or, in severe cases, lasting injury caused by rope pressing on nerve pathways, most commonly in the arm. The radial nerve runs close to the surface of the upper arm; compression here can cause "drop wrist," a temporary inability to lift the hand, which typically resolves within hours or days but can occasionally take longer. The rule is simple: never put rope directly on a nerve pathway, and check in constantly.

### Circulation

Rope that tightens — through movement, suspension, or knots that shift — can restrict blood flow. Learn to check for signs: fingers or toes that turn blue, white or numb; skin that feels cold; tingling or pins-and-needles. Any of these signals require immediate loosening. Always have safety scissors within immediate reach and know how to use them.

[image src="media/img/shibari-safety.jpg" alt="Shibari Safety"]

### Areas to Avoid

Some areas of the body should never have rope applied in ways that compress or constrict:

- **The neck** — never, under any circumstances
- **The front of the throat**
- **The popliteal fossa** (the back of the knee) — a major nerve bundle runs here
- **The inner wrist** — superficial blood vessels and nerves
- **Directly over joints** when bent under load

### Safe Words and Check-Ins

Use the same traffic light system described in the BDSM guide: **Red** to stop everything immediately, **Yellow** to slow down and adjust, **Green** to confirm all is well. In shibari, check-ins should be more frequent than in other forms of bondage because rope shifts as the body moves. Ask your bunny to move their fingers and report sensation every few minutes when learning new ties. If verbal communication is impaired, agree on a physical signal — dropping a held object, a specific hand gesture.

### The Emotional Dimension

Being bound produces powerful altered states. Some people experience *rope space* — a meditative, floaty, deeply relaxed state similar to a flow state — during tying. Others experience unexpected emotional responses: tears, laughter, fear or an overwhelming sense of peace. None of these is wrong. Aftercare (discussed at the end of this guide) is essential, not optional.

## Rope: What to Use

[image src="media/img/shibari-rope.jpg" alt="Shibari Rope Types"]

**Jute** is the traditional and most widely used material in shibari. It has a natural grip that holds knots well, becomes softer with use and conditioning, and has a sensory quality — its texture, smell and slight roughness against skin — that practitioners consider part of the experience. It requires conditioning with oil and occasional heat-treatment to prevent scratching, but it rewards the investment.

**Hemp** behaves similarly to jute and is slightly softer. Good for beginners who find jute too rough initially.

**Cotton** is the recommended starting material for absolute beginners. It is soft, forgiving, widely available and inexpensive. It does not hold knots as firmly as natural fibres and has less grip, which makes it less ideal for advanced work or suspension — but for ground-based learning ties, it is perfectly suitable and kind to skin.

**Nylon and MFP (Multifilament Polypropylene)** are synthetic alternatives, slippery and bright, often used for their visual impact. They do not hold knots as well, and the slipperiness that makes them look beautiful in photographs makes them more hazardous for beginners.

**Avoid:** dressing rope (too rough), electrical cord, climbing rope (too thick and stiff), or anything designed to stretch. Stretchy rope changes tension unpredictably and should never be used for bondage.

### Length and Quantity

A standard working length is **8 metres (approximately 26 feet)**. This is long enough for most single-column ties and basic body harnesses. Have a minimum of four to six lengths available before you begin — many ties require multiple bundles. A thickness of **6mm** is ideal for most work; 8mm is more comfortable for beginners.

## The Fundamental Knot: The Lark's Head

Almost all shibari begins by folding the rope in half to find its midpoint, then attaching it to the body or a structure using a lark's head (also called a *bight attach*). Every beginner should be able to tie this confidently before moving to full ties.

Fold your rope in half. The folded end is the *bight*. Pass the bight around the wrist, post, or object you are attaching to. Pass the two running ends of the rope through the bight and pull firmly. The rope is now anchored at its midpoint, with two working lengths to use.

## Beginner Ties

### 1. The Single Column Tie

[image src="media/img/shibari-single-column.jpg" alt="Single Column Tie"]

The single column tie is the foundation of all shibari. It is a safe, adjustable tie for a single limb — a wrist, ankle, thigh or upper arm — that will not tighten under load and can be checked and released quickly. Learn this one completely before attempting anything else.

**You will need:** one 8-metre length of rope.

**How to tie it:**

1. Find the midpoint of your rope (bight).
2. Wrap the rope around the wrist twice, leaving approximately 5cm of slack — you should be able to slide two fingers comfortably underneath.
3. Pass the bight between the two wraps and the wrist, then fold it back over the wraps.
4. Pass both working ends through the bight and pull to lock.
5. Tie off with a square knot or use the tails to connect to another anchor point.

**Test it:** ask your partner to pull against the tie. It should not tighten. If it does, untie and start again. A tie that tightens under load is unsafe.

---

### 2. The Double Column Tie

The double column tie binds two limbs together — both wrists, both ankles, or a wrist to an ankle — using the same principles as the single column tie, applied to two columns simultaneously. It is the natural next step after the single column.

**How to tie it:**

1. Find the midpoint of your rope.
2. Wrap around both columns (e.g. both wrists together) twice, leaving comfortable slack.
3. Pass the bight between the two columns, separating them, then fold it back over the wraps.
4. Pass both working ends through the bight and pull to lock.
5. Tie off as needed.

The tie between the two wraps acts as a cinch, preventing the columns from pressing together uncomfortably.

---

### 3. The Somerville Bowline

[image src="media/img/shibari-bowline.jpg" alt="Somerville Bowline"]

The Somerville bowline is considered by many practitioners to be the gold-standard beginner cuff because it is structurally impossible to tighten under load. It is slightly more complex to learn than the single column tie but rewards the effort with a level of safety that makes it the preferred tie for wrists and ankles in most circumstances.

Learning this tie from a video tutorial alongside written instructions is strongly recommended — the three-dimensional movement is easier to follow in motion. The principles remain the same: two wraps, a lock that cannot tighten, and a clean tie-off.

---

### 4. The Chest Harness (Ushiro Takatekote — Simplified)

[image src="media/img/shibari-chest-harness.jpg" alt="Chest Harness"]

The chest harness — in its traditional form called *ushiro takatekote* ("box tie") — is one of the most visually iconic ties in shibari, binding the arms behind the back within a structured rope frame across the chest and shoulders. A simplified, ground-based version is an appropriate first body harness.

**Safety note:** the full box tie, with arms folded behind the back, carries genuine risk of radial nerve compression if rope passes over the wrong part of the upper arm. Beginners should work with arms at the sides or use a simplified front harness until rope placement across the upper body is well understood.

**Simplified front chest harness:**

1. Begin at the midpoint of the rope at the sternum.
2. Wrap both working ends around the body above the breasts, cross them at the back and return them under the breasts, crossing again at the front.
3. Pass both ends upward between the two horizontal wraps, spreading the rope to create a clean band.
4. Tie off at the sternum or pass the ends over the shoulders and down the back.

This creates a clean, structured frame across the torso without any arm restraint, making it a safe and visually striking starting point.

## Intermediate Ties

These ties require confident execution of the foundational techniques above and a rigger who has practised the beginner ties many times on different bodies.

### The Takate Kote (Box Tie)

[image src="media/img/shibari-takatekote.jpg" alt="Takate Kote Box Tie"]

The takate kote is the most widely recognised shibari tie — the one seen in most photographs and demonstrations. It binds the arms behind the back in a doubled-forearm position within a structured chest harness, creating a symmetrical and visually dramatic result.

**Why it requires intermediate skill:** rope positioning across the upper arm — specifically avoiding the radial nerve in the spiral groove — must be precise and consistent. A poorly placed wrap is not merely uncomfortable; it can cause drop wrist. No beginner should attempt this tie on a partner until they can place and check rope on the upper arm reliably, know the warning signs of nerve compression, and have practised the component chest harness many times.

Done correctly, the takate kote is stable, adjustable and deeply comfortable to wear for extended periods. Done incorrectly, it is one of the most injury-prone ties in the practice. Study from an experienced practitioner before attempting it — in-person workshops are the gold standard.

---

### Hip and Leg Ties

**The Futomoto** (a thigh tie folding the lower leg against the upper leg) and **hip harnesses** are generally more forgiving than upper-body ties because there are fewer major nerve pathways at risk from correctly placed rope. They are accessible to intermediate riggers and produce striking visual results.

[image src="media/img/shibari-futomoto.png" alt="Futomoto Leg Tie"]

The popliteal fossa (the back of the knee) must always remain free of direct rope pressure. Otherwise, hip and leg ties are well suited to riggers who are ready to move beyond wrist and simple body ties.

## Advanced Ties and Suspension

[image src="media/img/shibari-suspension.jpg" alt="Shibari Suspension"]

Partial and full suspension — in which the person being tied is lifted partially or entirely off the ground by rope attached to an overhead point — represent the most visually spectacular and most technically demanding form of shibari. They are also the most dangerous.

Suspension concentrates enormous load on the rope, the attachment points and the body in ways that ground-based tying does not. Nerve compression risks multiply. Equipment failures carry serious consequences. The margin for error is small.

**Partial suspension** (one or both feet remaining on the ground, with rope taking some but not all of the body's weight) is the appropriate transition point from ground work. It allows a rigger to develop sensitivity to load, practice managing the bunny's position and weight, and identify problems before they become serious.

**Full suspension** should only be attempted by riggers with years of ground-based experience, comprehensive knowledge of anatomy and load-bearing mechanics, appropriate rigging hardware (rated suspension points, proper carabiners and rigging rings — never improvised), and ideally, direct mentorship from an experienced suspension rigger.

There is no shortcut to suspension. The rope community's consistent message is: **floor before air, always.** Practitioners who have injured themselves or their partners in suspension almost universally identify rushing past the floor work as the cause.

## Finding Community and Learning in Person

No written guide — however detailed — can replace learning shibari from an experienced practitioner in person. The rope community is generally welcoming, well organised and explicitly safety-focused.

**Rope jams** are informal community meetups where riggers and bunnies of all levels practice together. They are the most accessible entry point for beginners and the primary way the community shares knowledge. Most cities with an active kink or BDSM community have regular rope jams; search through local munch groups, FetLife, or dedicated rope communities online.

**Workshops** — from beginner half-days to multi-day intensives — are offered regularly by experienced practitioners and are the fastest way to build genuine competence. Learning to position rope correctly on someone else's body requires hands-on correction that written guides cannot provide.

**Photography and performance shibari** are distinct disciplines with their own communities, many of whom are as interested in the aesthetic and artistic dimensions as the erotic ones. Exploring these communities can provide a different perspective on what rope bondage is and can be.

## Aftercare in Shibari

Untying is part of the experience, not an afterthought. Take your time with it — being slowly unwrapped is often as intimate as being tied. As the rope comes off, watch the skin: rope marks are normal and expected, but look for anything unusual — deep indentation, unusual pallor, skin that doesn't bounce back.

**Rope marks** (red lines and patterns on the skin after removal) are normal, often beautiful, and typically fade within a few hours. They can be tender.

**Skin care after tying:** gently massaging the marked areas with a hydrating oil or lotion helps them fade and is itself a form of aftercare — grounding, warm and intimate.

**Sub drop and rope drop** can occur hours or even a day after a scene. The neurological come-down from the sustained altered state of rope space can produce sadness, flatness, anxiety or tearfulness that arrives unexpectedly. Knowing this in advance — and having agreed on what support looks like — makes it manageable rather than alarming.

**Rigger drop** is equally real. The intense concentration and responsibility of tying takes its own toll; riggers should expect their own version of the emotional come-down and plan for their own care accordingly.

The conversation before tying about what aftercare looks like for both of you is as important as any discussion of ties and techniques. Ask. Answer honestly. Take care of each other.

## A Final Word

Shibari at its best is a practice of attention — deep, unhurried, whole-body attention — that requires honesty, trust and a willingness to be genuinely present with another person. The rope is remarkable, but it is not the point. The point is what happens between two people when one of them holds the rope.

Start slow. Learn the foundations completely before advancing. Find community. Ask questions. Use your safe word whenever you need it. And enjoy the practice for exactly what it is: one of the most intimate things two people can do together.
