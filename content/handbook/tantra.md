## Tantra: A Practical Introduction

pic - tantra candle

Tantra is one of the most misunderstood words in modern sexuality. In its original context it referred to a sprawling set of spiritual traditions developed in India over centuries, most of which had little to do with sex. The Western, contemporary use of the word — sometimes called *neo-tantra* — focuses on a much narrower idea: that slowing sex down, paying close attention, and treating intimacy as a meditative practice produces an experience qualitatively different from ordinary sex.

This guide is about the practical version. Nothing in it requires spiritual belief. The techniques work because of how the human nervous system responds to slowness, attention, and breath — and they are accessible to any couple willing to spend an evening trying them.

---

## What Tantra Actually Does

Conventional sex tends to be goal-oriented. Arousal builds, intensifies, and resolves in orgasm. Tantra reverses the orientation. The goal is not orgasm but **presence** — a sustained state of intense connection in which arousal becomes a slow, continuous wave rather than a sharp climb.

The practical effects, reported consistently:

- Orgasms (when they happen) tend to be longer, deeper, and felt throughout the whole body rather than locally.
- Some practitioners learn to experience pleasure approaching orgasm without ejaculating or finishing — a state often described as more sustained than the orgasm itself.
- Sex becomes considerably longer; thirty to ninety minutes is a typical session.
- A sense of emotional intimacy with the partner that ordinary sex does not produce.

## Setting The Space

Tantra takes preparation seriously. The space matters because it shapes the quality of attention available within it.

- **Privacy and time.** Phones off. Door closed. At least an hour, ideally two.
- **Soft light.** Candles work well. Overhead light is too harsh.
- **Warmth.** The body relaxes more easily warm than cold.
- **Soft surfaces.** A bed is fine; a thick duvet on the floor is better for some of the seated practices.
- **No alcohol.** Subtle sensation matters. Even one drink dulls it.

This is not aesthetic indulgence. The slower and more attentive the practice, the more the environment affects it.

## Eye Gazing

The simplest tantric practice and the most surprising in its effect.

Sit facing each other, knees touching, fully clothed. Set a timer for five minutes. Look into your partner's eyes — one eye, not both — and do nothing else. No talking, no smiling on purpose, no looking away.

The first minute is often awkward. The second is often funny. By the third, something shifts. Most couples report a sense of seeing the other person fully for the first time in months or years. Some find it intensely emotional. Almost all report a noticeable increase in connection afterwards.

This is the foundation of most tantric practice and is worth doing on its own, without progressing to anything else, several times before incorporating it into longer sessions.

## Synchronised Breathing

Sit facing each other, close enough to touch. Place one hand on your own heart and one on your partner's. Breathe slowly — inhale for four counts, exhale for six — and try to match your partner's rhythm.

After a few minutes, switch to **alternating breathing**: as your partner exhales, you inhale, and vice versa. This produces a sense of exchange — *I am breathing what you have just breathed out* — that is unexpectedly intimate.

This is also useful within sex itself. Slowing down to breathe together for thirty seconds in the middle of an encounter resets the rhythm and intensifies what follows.

## The Yab-Yum Position

The classic tantric posture: one partner sits cross-legged; the other sits on their lap, wrapping their legs around the first partner's waist. The bodies are pressed together from hip to chest. Foreheads touching.

In this position, the focus is on **stillness**, not movement. Couples may stay in yab-yum for ten or fifteen minutes — breathing together, eye gazing, kissing slowly — without moving toward orgasm. Even when penetration is involved, the movement is slow and small. The intensity comes from sustained contact, not friction.

## Sensate Focus

Borrowed from sex therapy but consistent with tantric principles. One partner is the receiver; the other touches them slowly, deliberately, and without any agenda beyond the touch itself. The receiver's only job is to notice — temperature, pressure, texture — and to communicate when something feels particularly good or not.

Run for fifteen to twenty minutes per side. The receiver stays passive throughout. There is no expectation of arousal, of orgasm, or of reciprocation within the session.

Sensate focus dismantles the goal-orientation that most couples bring to sex by default. After a few sessions, the receiving and giving roles become more comfortable, the sensitivity to subtle sensation increases substantially, and a baseline of attentiveness carries over into ordinary sex.

## Slow Sex

Penetrative tantric sex follows from these foundations. The principles:

- **Slow entry.** Take a full minute. Pause when fully entered and simply hold the position.
- **Small, slow movements.** Often barely any movement at all. The receiver may rock gently; the giver may make tiny shifts of the hips. The intensity comes from the contact, not the thrust.
- **Eye contact maintained throughout.** This is the practice — eye contact is not a moment, it is the medium.
- **Pause often.** When arousal builds toward orgasm, stop completely. Breathe. Let the wave subside. Then resume.
- **Breath together.** Sync breathing whenever possible. Alternate when the rhythm allows.

Done this way, sex can sustain a high arousal plateau for thirty to sixty minutes without anyone reaching orgasm. When orgasm does eventually happen — for the couples who choose it — it is typically reported as fuller and longer than ordinary orgasm.

For some practitioners, the goal is to learn **non-ejaculatory orgasm** for men and **extended orgasm** for women — both well-documented possibilities that require practice and patience to develop.

## What Tantra Is Not

- It is not a guarantee of better sex. Some couples find it boring. That is a fair response.
- It is not a substitute for medical help with sexual dysfunction.
- It is not religious. The Western practical version makes no spiritual demands.
- It is not new-age fluff. The mechanisms — parasympathetic activation, sustained attention, breath regulation, oxytocin release through eye contact and slow touch — are all measurable and well-studied.

## A First Session

If you have never tried any of this, a simple way to begin:

1. **Prepare the space.** Twenty minutes of preparation. Soft light, warm room, no phones, no alcohol.
2. **Sit facing each other, fully clothed.** Five minutes of eye gazing.
3. **Synchronised breathing.** Five minutes, hands on hearts.
4. **Sensate focus.** Fifteen minutes one way, fifteen minutes the other. Clothing optional but slow.
5. **Yab-yum.** Ten minutes, no agenda.
6. **Whatever follows.** No expectation. Often, nothing more is needed.

Most couples who try this find it strikingly different from what they expected. Whether they continue is a matter of taste — but almost everyone who tries it once reports learning something about the other person that ordinary sex had not taught them.
