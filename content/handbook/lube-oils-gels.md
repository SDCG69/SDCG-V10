## Lube, Oils & Gels

[image src=“media/img/lube-header.jpg” alt=“Lubricants”]

Lubricant is one of the most underrated tools in a healthy sex life. For a significant number of people, it is the single thing that makes the difference between sex that is merely enjoyable and sex that is genuinely great. It reduces friction, increases comfort, enhances sensation, protects delicate tissue, and makes almost every sexual activity — solo or partnered — better.

And yet it carries a lingering stigma, as though needing it signals something wrong. It doesn’t. Natural lubrication varies enormously between people, fluctuates with hormones, medication, stress, arousal level, time of day, and age. Using lube is the same category of sensible decision as using sunscreen: it solves a problem before it becomes one.

## 1. Why Lube Matters

Natural vaginal lubrication is not a reliable indicator of arousal, desire, or attraction. It is a physiological process influenced by a huge range of factors — many of which have nothing to do with how turned on someone is. Menstrual cycle phase, contraceptive pills and hormonal coils, antidepressants and antihistamines, breastfeeding, perimenopause and menopause, dehydration, stress, and even alcohol can all reduce natural lubrication significantly.

For anal sex and play, lube is not optional — it is essential. The anus produces no natural lubrication of its own, and without adequate lube, friction causes microtears in tissue that are painful and that create infection risk.

For penetrative vaginal sex, lube reduces friction on both partners, which is more comfortable and — contrary to intuition — can actually increase sensation by allowing more movement and less drag.

For oral sex, flavoured or natural lubes can make extended sessions more pleasurable for the giver.

For solo sex and toy use, lube prolongs sessions, makes toy use more realistic, and protects tissue during friction-heavy activity.

## 2. Water-Based Lubricants

Water-based lubricants are the most widely used type, and for good reason: they are safe with all condoms and all sex toy materials, easy to wash off, and gentle on skin and mucous membranes.

[image src=“media/img/lube-waterbased.jpg” alt=“Water-Based Lubricants”]

**Pros:**

- Compatible with latex, polyurethane, and polyisoprene condoms
- Safe with all sex toy materials including silicone, glass, metal, and rubber
- Easy to wash off skin, fabric, and toys
- Available in a huge range of formulations, viscosities, and with added ingredients
- Generally the most affordable option
- Least likely to cause irritation

**Cons:**

- Absorb into the skin and dry out relatively quickly — require reapplication during longer sessions
- Not ideal for use in water (shower, bath, pool) as they dilute and wash away
- Some formulations contain glycerin, which can disrupt vaginal pH and cause yeast infections in sensitive individuals — check the ingredient list if you are prone to these

**Best for:** All-round use, condom-compatible sex, toy play, people with sensitive skin or prone to infections.

**What to look for:** Glycerin-free options if you are prone to yeast infections. Longer-lasting “gel” formulations for extended sessions. pH-balanced formulas specifically for vaginal use.

-----

## 3. Silicone-Based Lubricants

Silicone lubricants are long-lasting, extremely slippery, and waterproof. They are the preferred choice for many people for penetrative sex and particularly for anal play.

[image src=“media/img/lube-silicone.jpg” alt=“Silicone Lubricants”]

**Pros:**

- Last considerably longer than water-based — far less reapplication required
- Not diluted by water — excellent for shower or bath sex
- Very slippery with a distinct, luxurious feel
- Hypoallergenic — contain very few ingredients and are very unlikely to cause reactions
- Compatible with latex, polyurethane, and polyisoprene condoms

**Cons:**

- **Cannot be used with silicone sex toys** — silicone lube bonds with silicone toy material and damages the surface
- Harder to wash off — may require soap to fully remove from skin and definitely from fabric
- Can stain sheets — consider a dedicated sex blanket or towel

**Best for:** Shower or bath sex, anal play, extended sessions, people who find water-based lubes dry out too quickly.

**The silicone toy rule:** If you own silicone toys (and most quality modern toys are silicone), do not use silicone lube with them. Use water-based instead. The exception is if your toy is clearly labelled as non-silicone material (glass, metal, ABS plastic).

## 4. Oil-Based Lubricants

Oil-based lubricants — whether commercial formulations or natural oils — are long-lasting and feel excellent. They are also the most restricted in terms of compatibility.

**Pros:**

- Very long-lasting
- Great for massage that transitions into sex
- Excellent for external use, masturbation, and manual play
- Some natural options (coconut oil in particular) have additional benefits

**Cons:**

- **Degrade latex condoms** — dramatically increasing breakage risk. Do not use oil-based products with latex condoms under any circumstances.
- Can disrupt vaginal pH and flora with regular internal use, potentially increasing infection risk in susceptible people
- Difficult to wash off fully — can leave residue on skin and toys
- Not compatible with many sex toy materials

**Best for:** Massage, external play, masturbation (without condoms), partners who are both tested and using non-hormonal, non-barrier contraception.

## 5. Natural Oils

A number of natural oils are used as lubricants, some more appropriately than others.

[image src=“media/img/lube-natural.jpg” alt=“Natural Oils”]

**Coconut Oil**
The most popular natural lubricant. Solid at room temperature, liquid when warmed. Feels excellent, long-lasting, has mild antifungal properties, and smells pleasant. Widely tolerated. **However:** degrades latex condoms, and regular internal vaginal use can disrupt natural flora in some people. Test with a small amount first.

**Sweet Almond Oil**
Light, non-greasy, gentle on skin. Good for massage and external use. Same condom caveat applies.

**Jojoba Oil**
Technically a liquid wax, not an oil. Very stable, long shelf-life, closely resembles natural skin sebum. Gentle and very well-tolerated. Good for sensitive skin. Not latex-compatible.

**Oils to avoid:** Baby oil and petroleum jelly (Vaseline) are petroleum-based and should not be used internally. They are difficult to remove from genital tissue and can cause significant irritation and infection risk with extended use.

## 6. Specialty Lubricants

### Warming Lubes

Warming lubricants contain ingredients (typically glycerin, capsaicin derivatives, or niacin) that create a gentle warming sensation when applied to skin and mucous membranes. The warmth is usually activated by friction or blowing on the area.

They are popular for oral sex and external play. **Important:** Some people find warming lubes significantly irritating, particularly on sensitive genital tissue. Always do a small test on the inner wrist before internal use. Avoid formulations with capsaicin or “pepper” components for anything internal.

### Cooling/Tingling Lubes

Similar mechanism to warming lubes, but using menthol or similar compounds to create a cooling or tingling sensation. The sensation can be unexpectedly intense for some people — again, test before internal use.

### Flavoured Lubes

Flavoured lubes are water-based, designed primarily for oral sex. They make extended oral sessions much more pleasurable for the giver and come in an enormous variety of flavours (strawberry, cherry, chocolate, mint, and many more).

**Check the ingredients** if you are prone to yeast infections — most flavoured lubes contain sugar or glycerin, which can disrupt vaginal pH. Use externally on the genitals for oral sex rather than inserting internally.

### Anal Lubes

Anal-specific lubricants tend to be thicker in consistency — gel rather than liquid — to stay in place and provide sustained cushioning. They are generally water-based or silicone-based (check toy compatibility). Some contain mild desensitising agents.

**On desensitising agents:** Anal lubes that numb the area are popular but controversial in the kink community. The mild discomfort of anal play is a useful signal — if something hurts, it is telling you to slow down, use more lube, or stop. Numbing that signal can allow damage to occur without awareness. If you choose to use a desensitising product, go very slowly and pay attention to your partner’s non-verbal responses carefully.

### Sensitising Lubes

Sensitising lubes claim to heighten sensation — often marketed as “arousal gels” or “stimulating lubes.” They typically contain L-arginine (which increases blood flow) or gentle irritants. Many people find them genuinely effective; others find them irritating. Test small amounts first.

## 7. Compatibility Guide

This is the most important practical section of this guide. Using the wrong lube with a condom or toy can cause real problems.

|Lubricant Type                    |Latex Condoms   |Polyurethane / Polyisoprene Condoms|Silicone Toys    |Other Toy Materials|
|----------------------------------|----------------|-----------------------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
|**Water-Based**                   |✅ Safe          |✅ Safe                             |✅ Safe           |✅ Safe             |
|**Silicone-Based**                |✅ Safe          |✅ Safe                             |❌ Damages surface|✅ Safe             |
|**Oil-Based / Natural Oils**      |❌ Degrades latex|✅ Safe                             |✅ Safe           |Check manufacturer |
|**Petroleum (Vaseline, Baby Oil)**|❌ Degrades latex|❌ Avoid                            |❌ Avoid          |❌ Avoid            |

**Quick rules to remember:**

- Water-based: works with everything, every time.
- Silicone: works with condoms and non-silicone toys. Not with silicone toys.
- Oils: works with polyurethane/polyisoprene condoms. **Never with latex condoms.**
- Petroleum products: avoid for sexual use.

## 8. How to Use Lube Well

[image src=“media/img/lube-why.jpg” alt=“Why Lube Matters”]

**Use more than you think you need.** The most common mistake is using too little. Lube should feel genuinely slippery. A few drops is rarely enough.

**Reapply freely.** Water-based lubes in particular need reapplication during extended sessions. Keep the bottle nearby. Reapplying is not an interruption — make it part of play.

**Apply to both surfaces.** For penetration, apply lube both externally and on whatever is being inserted. For toy use, apply to the toy and to the body.

**Warm it up.** Cold lube from the bottle can be jarring. Warm a small amount between your palms for a few seconds first, or keep the bottle somewhere slightly warm.

**Communicate.** Ask your partner if they’d like more lube. Normalise it as a regular part of sex rather than something only produced when there’s a “problem.” The more casually it is integrated, the better.

**Storage:** Keep lube away from direct sunlight and extreme temperatures. Check expiry dates — particularly important for lubes with more complex formulations.
