## Exploring Sexuality: A Guide for LGBTQ+ People

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-intro.jpg" alt="LGBTQ+ Sexuality Guide"]

Sexuality and gender are among the most fundamental aspects of human experience, and for LGBTQ+ people — lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer and the full spectrum of identities beyond — the journey of understanding and expressing them carries particular weight. It happens in a world that has not always been welcoming, within families that may not understand, and often without the roadmaps that heterosexual and cisgender people can take for granted.

This guide exists to provide those roadmaps. It covers each identity and experience within the LGBTQ+ spectrum in depth — not as a clinical taxonomy, but as a practical, honest resource for people at any stage of their journey: questioning, newly out, long settled in their identity, or simply wanting to understand.

Nothing in this guide assumes a starting point. Whether you are reading it at sixteen, having just felt something you cannot name, or at fifty, returning to questions you put aside decades ago, it is written for you.

---

## Understanding Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity

Before exploring specific identities, two foundational concepts need to be distinguished — not because they are complicated, but because confusing them causes genuine harm to people who are working out who they are.

**Sexual orientation** describes who you are attracted to — romantically, sexually, or both. It is about the direction of desire, not its nature or intensity.

**Gender identity** describes who you are — your internal sense of your own gender, which may or may not correspond to the sex you were assigned at birth.

These are entirely separate axes. A transgender woman can be heterosexual, lesbian, bisexual, asexual or anything else. A gay man's gender identity is not in question because of his sexuality. A non-binary person may experience attraction in any direction. The two concepts operate independently and must be understood that way.

Both are distinct from **gender expression** — the way a person presents their gender through clothing, behaviour, name, pronouns and appearance — and from **biological sex**, which refers to chromosomes, hormones and anatomy. None of these four things necessarily predicts the others.

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-spectrum.jpg" alt="LGBTQ+ Spectrum"]

### The Kinsey Scale and Beyond

In 1948, Alfred Kinsey proposed that sexual attraction exists on a continuum from exclusively heterosexual to exclusively homosexual, with most people falling somewhere in between. This was a significant departure from the binary thinking of its time and remains useful as a starting point. But it has two significant limitations: it only addresses attraction to men and women, and it treats sexuality as a fixed point on a line rather than something that can shift across contexts, partners and time.

More recent frameworks — including the Klein Sexual Orientation Grid, which maps attraction across fantasy, behaviour, emotional preference, social preference, lifestyle and self-identification — offer a more complete picture. The practical implication is the same: human sexuality is wider, more varied and more fluid than most people are initially taught, and the label that fits you today may not be the only or final label that ever will.

## Gay Men

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-gay-men.jpg" alt="Gay Men"]

Gay men — men who experience sexual and romantic attraction primarily or exclusively to other men — represent one of the most visible and culturally rich communities within the LGBTQ+ spectrum. Gay culture has produced distinctive art, language, community structures and modes of intimacy that have developed, in significant part, through the shared experience of navigating a world that was not always safe.

### Identity and Coming Out

For many gay men, the experience of recognising their sexuality involves a period of misalignment: feeling attraction that doesn't match what is expected, often long before the vocabulary to name it is available. This period — sometimes called the "questioning" phase — can be isolating, particularly for men who grew up in religious, culturally conservative or rural environments where gay identity was invisible or explicitly condemned.

Coming out — the ongoing process of disclosing one's sexuality to others — is not a single event but a continuous series of decisions made across a lifetime. A man comes out to himself first (often the hardest), then to trusted friends, family, colleagues and new acquaintances, each time assessing risk and reward anew. There is no obligation to come out to everyone, at any particular time, in any particular way. The decision belongs entirely to the individual.

The phrase "coming out of the closet" implies a single decisive moment, but most gay men describe a process that unfolds across years, in stages, with different people. Being out to close friends and not to family, or out socially but not professionally, is extremely common and entirely valid.

### Sex and Intimacy Between Men

Sex between men takes as many forms as sex between any other combination of people. The focus on anal sex in cultural representations of gay sex reflects a partial and often misleading picture. Many gay men do not have anal sex at all, or not as a primary or preferred activity. Oral sex, manual stimulation, frottage (body-to-body friction), mutual masturbation, and a wide range of other acts constitute fulfilling and complete sexual experiences for many.

For those who do engage in anal sex, the guide elsewhere in this series covers preparation, technique and safety in full detail. A few additional points are specific to sex between men:

**Positions and roles** are often described in the language of "top" (penetrating partner), "bottom" (receiving partner) and "versatile" (comfortable with both). These are useful shorthand but are not fixed identities — many men's preferences shift with partners, time and experience, and there is no hierarchy of value between roles.

**Frottage and outercourse** — body-to-body rubbing, intercrural sex (between the thighs) and other forms of non-penetrative intimacy — are both pleasurable and lower-risk from an STI perspective, and are underrepresented in discussions of sex between men.

**The prostate** — described in detail in the anal sex guide — is a source of deep, distinctive pleasure accessible via anal penetration or firm external pressure on the perineum. Many men, gay and straight alike, have never explored this despite its significant potential.

### Sexual Health for Gay and Bisexual Men

Gay and bisexual men face specific sexual health considerations that differ from those affecting the general population, primarily because receptive anal sex is the highest-risk sexual activity for HIV transmission.

**HIV** remains a significant concern, though the landscape has changed dramatically. An undetectable viral load in an HIV-positive person means they cannot transmit the virus to partners — this is the U=U principle (Undetectable = Untransmittable), confirmed by large-scale clinical evidence. Treatment is highly effective and widely available.

**PrEP** (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis) — a daily antiretroviral medication — reduces the risk of acquiring HIV by over 99% when taken consistently. It is available free on the NHS in England, Scotland and Wales, via sexual health clinics.

**PEP** (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis) is emergency medication taken within 72 hours of a potential HIV exposure. It must be started as soon as possible — within 24 hours is ideal — and is available from sexual health clinics and A&E departments.

**Regular testing** is the cornerstone of sexual health for sexually active gay and bisexual men. STIs including gonorrhoea, chlamydia, syphilis, herpes and hepatitis can be transmitted via oral and anal sex, often without symptoms. Testing every three months is recommended for men with multiple partners; the NHS offers free home testing kits via SH:24 and similar services, as well as walk-in clinic appointments.

**Mpox (formerly monkeypox)** — vaccination is available and recommended for gay, bisexual and men who have sex with men (MSM) with multiple partners, via sexual health clinics and GP practices.

**Hepatitis A and B** — both transmissible via sexual contact. Vaccination is free and strongly recommended.

### Community and Culture

Gay male culture — centred in bars, clubs, Pride events, community organisations, online spaces and informal networks — is among the most developed and distinctive within the LGBTQ+ spectrum. Terms including bear (larger, often hairier gay men who celebrate a particular masculine aesthetic), otter, cub, twink, daddy, leather, and many others describe subcommunities and aesthetics that have developed their own social norms, spaces and sometimes sexual cultures.

These subcommunities are not taxonomies to be placed in — they are starting points for finding where you feel at home. Many gay men move between communities throughout their lives and find their sense of belonging shifts accordingly.

## Lesbian Women

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-lesbian.jpg" alt="Lesbian Women"]

Lesbian women — women who experience sexual and romantic attraction primarily or exclusively to other women — share with gay men the experience of same-sex attraction while navigating a distinct set of cultural contexts, community histories and specific considerations.

### Identity and Experience

Lesbian identity is sometimes described as invisible in a way gay male identity is not — a phenomenon sometimes called "lesbian erasure." Two women together are more likely to be assumed to be friends; lesbian sexuality is more frequently sexualised for the male gaze rather than recognised on its own terms; and women questioning their sexuality often face the additional layer of internalised messaging that female desire is inherently more fluid and therefore less "real" or fixed.

The result is that many lesbian women come to their identity later than gay men on average, though this is a generalisation with significant individual variation. Some women have known since childhood; others have lived as heterosexual, including in marriages and with children, before recognising and acting on their attraction to women. Both experiences are valid and neither is less authentic.

**Bisexual women** who are currently in relationships with women may or may not identify as lesbian; many identify as bisexual regardless of the gender of their current partner, recognising that identity reflects the full pattern of one's attraction rather than its current expression.

### Sex and Intimacy Between Women

Sex between women is one of the most comprehensively misrepresented areas of human sexuality — distorted both by pornographic representation, which tends to reflect male fantasy rather than female experience, and by a cultural silence that treats lesbian sex as somehow less complete or serious than penetrative heterosexual sex.

Sex between women is as varied, complex and fulfilling as sex between any other combination of people. Common acts include:

**Oral sex (cunnilingus)** is among the most commonly reported forms of sex between women and is described by many as the most reliably orgasmic. The clitoris — over 8,000 nerve endings in the external tip alone, extending internally in a wishbone shape — responds to direct and indirect stimulation of the glans, hood, shaft and surrounding tissue. Technique, rhythm, pressure and variation matter considerably more than any single specific movement, and communication with a partner about what feels good is the most reliable guide.

**Manual stimulation** — fingers used externally on the vulva and clitoris, or internally — is deeply versatile. The G-spot (on the front vaginal wall, roughly 5–7cm in) and the A-spot (deeper, toward the cervix) are accessible via curved fingers with a "come-hither" motion toward the navel. Simultaneous clitoral stimulation — external and internal together — is how the majority of people with vulvas reliably reach orgasm.

**Tribadism (scissoring, grinding)** — vulva-to-vulva contact — produces stimulation for both partners simultaneously and is more common in practice than its representation in media would suggest. It requires some experimentation with position and angle to find what works for both bodies.

**Toys and strap-ons** are used by many couples — a strap-on harness with a dildo allows one partner to penetrate the other vaginally or anally. Double-ended dildos can provide simultaneous penetration. Vibrators — external, internal, or both — are widely used both solo and partnered. There is no hierarchy in which sex with a toy is "less real" than sex without one; the goal is pleasure, and tools that enhance it are simply tools.

**Fingernails** should be trimmed and filed before any manual vaginal or anal stimulation — even a small edge can cause a painful tear in sensitive tissue.

### Sexual Health for Lesbian and Bisexual Women

A persistent myth holds that sex between women carries minimal STI risk. This is false. STIs including herpes, HPV, gonorrhoea, chlamydia, trichomonas and syphilis can all be transmitted between women via oral sex, manual contact, shared toys, and skin-to-skin genital contact.

**Dental dams** — thin sheets of latex or polyurethane used as a barrier during oral sex or rimming — reduce transmission risk effectively. They are available from sexual health clinics and some pharmacies; cutting open a condom lengthwise provides an equivalent barrier.

**Shared sex toys** should either be washed thoroughly between uses or covered with a new condom for each partner.

**Cervical screening** (smear tests) is recommended for all people with a cervix aged 25–64, regardless of sexual orientation. Lesbian and bisexual women are significantly less likely to attend screening — partly due to the mistaken belief that it is only relevant for people who have sex with men. HPV can be transmitted between women and is the primary cause of cervical cancer.

**Regular STI testing** is recommended for sexually active lesbian and bisexual women, including for HIV — though transmission risk between female partners is lower than in male-to-male or male-to-female sex, it is not zero.

### Community and Culture

Lesbian community has its own rich cultural history — from the Daughters of Bilitis (the first lesbian civil rights organisation, founded in the US in 1955) to the specific lesbian feminist movements of the 1970s and 80s to contemporary queer spaces that are less strictly defined. Terms including butch (a lesbian who presents with a more traditionally masculine gender expression), femme (a more traditionally feminine presentation), stem (stud/femme), and many others describe identities and aesthetics within lesbian culture, though their use is personal and no one is obligated to claim any of them.

Lesbian bars and specifically lesbian spaces have declined significantly in number since the 1990s, a trend often attributed to the rise of online dating and the broadening of LGBTQ+ spaces. Many women find community through queer nights, sports teams, online groups, book clubs and Pride events rather than in dedicated venues.

## Bisexual and Pansexual People

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-bisexual.jpg" alt="Bisexual and Pansexual"]

Bisexuality — broadly defined as attraction to more than one gender — is the most common sexual orientation within the LGBTQ+ spectrum, yet bisexual people are among the least visible and most poorly served by both mainstream culture and the LGBTQ+ community itself.

### Defining Bisexuality

The simplest working definition of bisexuality is attraction to people of your own gender and people of other genders. This is sometimes refined as "attraction to same and different," deliberately avoiding the implication that only two genders exist. The Bisexual Manifesto of 1990 stated it clearly: "Do not assume that bisexuality is binary or duogamous in nature."

Bisexual attraction is not necessarily equal in intensity toward different genders, nor is it necessarily simultaneous — it is a pattern of attraction across a lifetime, not a requirement to feel equal desire for all genders at all times. A bisexual person in a long-term relationship with someone of one gender does not cease to be bisexual; their identity reflects the totality of their attraction, not the gender of their current partner.

**Pansexuality** — attraction to people regardless of gender, or attraction in which gender is not a determining factor — is a related but distinct identity claimed by many people for whom bisexuality feels too binary or insufficiently inclusive of non-binary and trans genders. In practice, pansexuality and bisexuality overlap significantly and many people use them interchangeably or move between them.

**Polysexuality** describes attraction to multiple but not all genders, and sits in similar territory to bisexuality and pansexuality without implying attraction to any particular set.

### Biphobia and Bisexual Erasure

Bisexual people face a specific form of discrimination — biphobia — that operates distinctly from homophobia. It includes the persistent belief that bisexuality is not a real orientation but rather a phase, a form of confusion, greediness, or a stepping stone to fully gay or straight identity. These stereotypes come from both heterosexual society and, frequently, from within gay and lesbian communities.

The consequences are significant. Research consistently shows that bisexual people report worse mental health outcomes than both heterosexual and gay or lesbian people — higher rates of depression, anxiety, and suicide — linked in large part to the experience of being rejected or invalidated on all sides. Bisexual people are also less likely to be out to family and friends and have smaller social support networks, both of which compound the mental health impact.

The antidote to bisexual erasure is validation — of others and of oneself. Bisexuality is a legitimate, complete identity that does not require external validation, a certain number of partners of different genders, or any other proof.

### Relationships and Sexuality

Bisexual people have the same range of relationship options as anyone else: monogamous, non-monogamous, polyamorous, casual, long-term. The specific sexology of a bisexual relationship depends on the genders of the people in it, not on any particular "bisexual way" of having sex.

Some bisexual people find that their attractions have different qualities toward different genders — not stronger or weaker but differently textured, engaging different aspects of their desire. This is a normal feature of bisexuality rather than a reason to question the identity itself.

## Transgender People

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-trans.jpg" alt="Transgender People"]

Transgender people are those whose gender identity differs from the sex they were assigned at birth. This is one of the most misunderstood and misrepresented areas of LGBTQ+ experience, and one of the most important to understand clearly.

### What Being Transgender Means

Gender identity — the internal, deeply felt sense of one's own gender — is distinct from biological sex, gender expression, and sexual orientation. For transgender people, the gender identity they experience does not match the sex assigned at birth (male or female, typically based on external anatomy). This mismatch — called gender dysphoria when it causes significant distress — is the defining feature of trans experience, though not all trans people experience dysphoria in the same way or to the same degree.

**Trans women** are women who were assigned male at birth. **Trans men** are men who were assigned female at birth. **Non-binary people** — discussed in their own section below — have gender identities that do not fit neatly into the categories of man or woman.

Being transgender is not a mental illness. It was removed from the category of mental disorders by the World Health Organisation in 2019, reclassified instead as a condition related to sexual health. It is not caused by upbringing, trauma, social influence, or any other external factor — though the age and context in which a person recognises and can act on their gender identity is shaped by all of these.

### Transition

Transition refers to the range of steps a transgender person may take to bring their external life into alignment with their gender identity. It is not a single event or a defined checklist — it is a process that looks different for every person and may include any combination of the following:

**Social transition** — changing name, pronouns, and gender expression (clothing, hair, presentation). Social transition has no medical component and can be undertaken at any age without clinical involvement. For many trans people — especially children and young people — social transition alone is sufficient, or is the first step taken while other options are considered.

**Medical transition** — which may include hormone therapy (HRT) and surgery, and is available via NHS gender identity clinics (GICs), private providers, or the informed consent model now available through some GP practices.

*Hormone therapy* for trans women typically involves oestrogen and anti-androgens, which over months and years produce feminising effects: redistribution of body fat, softening of skin, breast development, reduction of body and facial hair. For trans men, testosterone produces masculinising effects: a lower voice, increased muscle mass, redistribution of body fat, facial and body hair growth, cessation of menstrual periods, and clitoral growth.

*Surgical options* vary widely. For trans women, these may include vaginoplasty (creation of a vagina), orchiectomy (removal of the testes), facial feminisation surgery and breast augmentation. For trans men, options include mastectomy (chest reconstruction), hysterectomy, and phalloplasty or metoidioplasty (creation of a penis). No trans person is required to pursue any surgery; many do not. A person is no less transgender for choosing not to have surgery.

**Legal transition** — changing name by deed poll (free, straightforward, no legal requirement for medical involvement) and applying for a Gender Recognition Certificate (GRC) in the UK. The GRC changes the legal gender on a birth certificate. NHS treatment and other services should be provided according to a person's lived gender regardless of whether they hold a GRC.

### Sexuality and Trans People

Trans people experience the full range of sexual orientations. A trans woman may be attracted to men, women, both, neither, or anyone regardless of gender. Her sexual orientation is not determined by or changed by the fact of her being trans, and it is not the same thing as her gender identity.

Trans people also have physical bodies that deserve specific care and attention in sexual health and intimacy contexts.

**Trans women who have not had genital surgery** have a penis and testes, which may or may not play a role in their sex life. Some trans women experience significant dysphoria around their genitals and prefer not to have them involved in sex; others do not. Communication with a partner — without assumption in either direction — is essential. HRT typically reduces erectile function and changes the nature of orgasm; some trans women experience orgasm without erection and with reduced ejaculate.

**Trans women who have had vaginoplasty** have a vagina (created typically from penile or scrotal tissue) that requires specific care. Most techniques require ongoing dilation to maintain vaginal depth — a process explained in full during surgical aftercare. The neovagina does not self-lubricate in the same way as a natal vagina; lubrication is always needed. Sensation varies between individuals and tends to improve over time as nerve endings reconnect.

**Trans men who have not had chest surgery** may experience dysphoria around their chests. Many use a binder — a garment that compresses breast tissue to create a flat chest — and have specific preferences about whether their chest is touched during sex. Never touch a trans man's chest without explicit permission if you are uncertain of their preferences.

**Trans men who have not had genital surgery** have a vagina and, typically after testosterone, a significantly enlarged clitoris (which may be referred to by many different terms — ask and use the terms your partner prefers). The vagina remains functional for penetrative sex and produces natural lubrication, though testosterone may reduce lubrication somewhat. Testosterone also increases libido significantly for many trans men.

**Trans men who have had phalloplasty** have a constructed penis (created typically from arm, thigh or abdominal tissue) that is capable of sensation and, with an internal pump prosthesis, erection. Sensation varies significantly between surgical techniques and individuals.

**The most important principle across all of this:** ask. Never assume what a trans partner's body is, what they call it, what role they want it to play in sex, or what they are comfortable with. A trans person who wants to have sex with you has already made that decision; treat them with the same curiosity, care and communication you would bring to any other partner.

### Sexual Health Considerations for Trans People

Trans people are frequently poorly served by sexual health services that are not trained or equipped to account for their specific needs. Some specific considerations:

**Cervical screening** — trans men with a cervix should continue to attend cervical screening. They are significantly less likely to do so and may face services ill-equipped to accommodate them sensitively. GP practices should be able to facilitate screening regardless of the gender marker on a patient's record.

**Prostate screening** — trans women who were assigned male at birth retain their prostate gland, even after vaginoplasty. Ongoing prostate awareness is relevant, though HRT reduces risk. Anal examination and PSA testing remain relevant.

**STI risk and testing** — applicable based on the activities engaged in and the anatomy involved, regardless of gender identity. Trans people should be able to access sexual health services without being required to disclose trans status, though informing a clinician may enable more appropriate care.

**HRT interactions** — some medications interact with HRT. Always inform a prescribing clinician of HRT use.

## Non-Binary and Genderqueer People

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-nonbinary.jpg" alt="Non-Binary People"]

Non-binary is an umbrella term for gender identities that do not fit within the conventional binary of man and woman. It is one of the fastest-growing areas of gender identity in survey data, particularly among younger generations — not because non-binary identities are new, but because the language to name them, and the cultural permission to claim them, has become more widely available.

### What Non-Binary Means

Non-binary is not a single identity but a range of them, all of which fall outside the binary. These include:

**Genderqueer** — an identity outside the binary, often implying a degree of queerness in relation to gender norms.

**Agender** — the experience of having no gender, or of gender being irrelevant or absent from one's sense of self.

**Bigender** — the experience of having two distinct gender identities, which may be felt simultaneously or at different times.

**Genderfluid** — gender that shifts over time, between or across genders.

**Demigender** — partial identification with one gender alongside other gender experiences; includes demigirl, demiboy and others.

**Two-Spirit** — a term specific to some Indigenous North American cultures, describing a person who embodies both masculine and feminine spiritual qualities. This term belongs to those cultures and should not be used to describe non-Indigenous non-binary people.

Many non-binary people also identify as transgender, since their gender identity differs from the sex assigned at birth. Many do not — identity is a personal matter and there is no correct way to categorise oneself.

### Pronouns and Language

Many non-binary people use pronouns other than he/him or she/her. The most widely used is **they/them** (singular) — a grammatically established usage in English that has been used in this sense for centuries. Other pronouns used by non-binary people include ze/zir, xe/xem, and others. Some people use multiple sets of pronouns.

Using a person's correct pronouns is a fundamental act of respect, not a matter of opinion. Misgendering — using incorrect pronouns or a name that a person no longer uses — is painful, regardless of intent. If you make a mistake, correct yourself briefly, apologise once, and move on without extended dwelling on the error.

### Sex, Intimacy and the Body

Non-binary people have bodies as varied as any other population, and the relevance of any body part to their sexuality and sense of self is entirely individual. Some non-binary people are comfortable with and use standard anatomical language for their bodies; others use different terms, and many have strong preferences about how their body is engaged with during sex.

The cardinal rule — applicable to all transgender and gender-diverse people — is to ask without assuming. What does your partner call this part of their body? What do they want you to do, or not do, with it? These conversations, handled with genuine curiosity and care, are among the most intimate available.

Non-binary people may identify their sexuality in any number of ways — gay, bisexual, pansexual, queer, heterosexual, asexual, or without a label — and their sexual orientation is not determined by their gender identity.

## Queer Identity

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-queer.jpg" alt="Queer Identity"]

Queer — once a term of abuse, reclaimed by LGBTQ+ communities from the late 1980s onward — now functions both as a specific identity and as a broader political and cultural descriptor.

As a specific identity, "queer" is used by people who experience sexuality and/or gender outside the norms of heterosexuality and cisgender identity but who do not feel that more specific labels (gay, lesbian, bisexual, trans) fully capture their experience. It signals a rejection of rigid categorisation in favour of a more fluid, self-determined relationship with identity. Some people use "queer" because their sexuality or gender is genuinely difficult to pin down within existing categories; others use it as a political stance as much as a personal description.

As a cultural and political term, "queer" describes a tradition of thought and activism that questions and challenges normative structures around gender and sexuality — not merely seeking inclusion within existing institutions but interrogating those institutions themselves.

The word carries different weight for different people. For those who experienced it as a weapon — particularly older LGBTQ+ people — it may remain painful. Never use it to describe someone without knowing they use it themselves.

## Asexual and Aromantic Spectrum

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-asexual.jpg" alt="Asexual and Aromantic"]

Asexuality and the aromantic spectrum represent the low or absent end of sexual and romantic attraction — areas of human experience as legitimate and as deserving of understanding and community as any other.

### Asexuality

An asexual person experiences little or no sexual attraction to others. This is a sexual orientation, not a disorder, a choice, a symptom of trauma, a phase, or a result of "not having met the right person." Asexuality is not the same as celibacy — celibacy is a behavioural choice; asexuality is an orientation.

The asexual spectrum (often called "the ace spectrum") includes:

**Asexual (ace)** — experiencing no or minimal sexual attraction.

**Demisexual** — experiencing sexual attraction only after forming a significant emotional bond with a person. Demisexual people may experience attraction rarely and only in specific relational contexts.

**Graysexual (gray-ace)** — experiencing sexual attraction infrequently, weakly, or only under specific circumstances. Gray-asexuality occupies the space between asexual and sexual on the spectrum.

Asexual people may still experience:
- **Romantic attraction** — a desire for romantic partnership without sexual involvement
- **Aesthetic attraction** — finding people visually or aesthetically appealing without sexual attraction
- **Sensual attraction** — a desire for non-sexual physical closeness (hugging, cuddling, hand-holding)
- **Sexual drive** (libido) — a physical desire for sexual release that is not directed toward another person

These distinctions matter because they are frequently confused. An asexual person may masturbate, may be in a romantic relationship, may want physical affection, and may enjoy sex with a partner even without experiencing attraction — each of these is consistent with asexual identity.

### The Aromantic Spectrum

Romantic attraction — the desire for a romantic relationship, as distinct from sexual attraction — also exists on a spectrum. Aromantic people experience little or no romantic attraction and may have little interest in romantic relationships, regardless of their sexual orientation.

**Aromantic (aro)** — experiencing no or minimal romantic attraction.

**Demiromantic** — experiencing romantic attraction only after an emotional bond forms.

**Grayromantic** — experiencing romantic attraction rarely or weakly.

Sexual and romantic orientations operate independently. A person can be:
- **Asexual and aromantic** — experiencing neither sexual nor romantic attraction
- **Asexual and romantic** — experiencing romantic attraction without sexual attraction (e.g. a heteromantic asexual person wants romantic relationships with people of a different gender but not sex)
- **Sexual and aromantic** — experiencing sexual attraction without romantic interest
- Any combination thereof

### Relationships on the Ace and Aro Spectrum

Asexual people navigate relationships with the same complexity as anyone else, and often with the additional challenge of communicating their needs to partners who may not share or initially understand their orientation.

Relationships where one partner is asexual and one is not — sometimes called "mixed relationships" — require honest communication about what each person needs and what they are comfortable with. Some asexual people are willing to have sex for a partner's benefit; others are not and should not be pressured. Some asexual people find sexual activity aversive; others are neutral or mildly positive toward it. None of these positions is more or less authentically asexual.

The asexual community has developed rich concepts for relationships that fall outside conventional romantic-sexual pairing — including **queerplatonic relationships**: partnerships that are emotionally intimate, committed and significant without being romantic in the conventional sense. The vocabulary of asexual communities is useful for anyone wanting to think carefully about the shapes relationships can take.

## Intersex People

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-intersex.jpg" alt="Intersex"]

Intersex refers to people born with biological characteristics — chromosomal, gonadal, hormonal or anatomical — that do not fit the conventional definitions of male or female bodies. It is estimated that between 1.7% and 4% of the population is intersex in some form, making it roughly as common as red hair.

Intersex is distinct from transgender identity (which concerns gender identity, not biological sex characteristics) and from sexual orientation. An intersex person may be transgender or not; may be gay, straight, bisexual or any orientation; may identify as male, female, non-binary or any other gender.

The most significant issue affecting intersex people is the medical practice of performing surgeries on intersex infants and children to make their bodies conform to binary sex norms — surgeries that are non-consensual, often irreversible, and that many intersex adults report as harmful and traumatic. Major medical organisations have moved toward recommending delay of non-urgent surgeries until individuals are old enough to consent, though practice varies significantly.

Intersex people have bodies that are as sexual and worthy of pleasure as any others. The specific anatomy varies enormously depending on the intersex variation; no assumption should be made about what an intersex person's body is like or how it works. Curiosity, respect and communication are the only universally appropriate approach.

## Coming Out: A Practical Guide

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-coming-out.jpg" alt="Coming Out"]

Coming out is simultaneously deeply personal and universal within LGBTQ+ experience. Nearly every LGBTQ+ person navigates it; no two people navigate it the same way. What follows is a practical framework rather than a prescribed sequence.

### Coming Out to Yourself

For many people, this is the most significant step and the hardest. It requires overcoming internalised stigma — the internalised homophobia, transphobia or biphobia absorbed from a world that has communicated, explicitly or implicitly, that certain identities are inferior, shameful or wrong. This messaging operates below the level of conscious belief and can persist even in people who intellectually accept LGBTQ+ identity without question.

There is no obligation to have a definitive label. "I am attracted to women, and I'm not sure what that means yet" is a complete and valid place to be. Sitting with uncertainty rather than forcing premature certainty is often more honest and ultimately more helpful than claiming a label before it fits.

Journalling, online communities (Reddit's r/lgbt, r/questioning, r/asexuality and many others), and LGBTQ+ focused therapy are all useful supports during this phase.

### Assessing Safety

Before coming out to others, a realistic assessment of safety is necessary — not to discourage disclosure, but to ensure it is made thoughtfully.

For young people in unsupportive households, early disclosure to parents can carry real risks: rejection, withdrawal of financial support, in some cases being asked to leave home. This does not mean hiding indefinitely, but it does mean that the timing of disclosure matters and that building a support network of accepting people before disclosing to high-risk family members is a reasonable and protective strategy.

**Resources if safety is a concern:** the Albert Kennedy Trust (UK) supports LGBTQ+ young people facing homelessness or family rejection. Switchboard (0300 330 0630) provides a listening service. Mermaids supports trans and gender-diverse young people and their families.

### Choosing How and When

There is no universal correct way to come out. Written communication — a letter, a text, a message — allows the person coming out to say exactly what they want without interruption and gives the recipient time to process before responding. An in-person conversation allows for immediate connection and reassurance. Neither is better; each has appropriate contexts.

It is entirely legitimate to come out to one person but not another, to be out in some areas of life but not others, to come out and then choose not to discuss it further. Disclosure is not an obligation, and being out does not mean being a perpetual educator or explainer.

### Managing the Response

Not all coming-out conversations go as hoped. A response that is shocked, confused, dismissive or explicitly rejecting is painful regardless of its source, and pain is the appropriate response to it — not evidence that the disclosure was a mistake.

Unhelpful early responses — "are you sure?", "it's probably a phase", "you don't look gay" — often come from people who will eventually come to acceptance. Research on family responses to LGBTQ+ disclosure shows that for the majority of families, initial negative responses improve significantly over time, particularly with sustained, honest communication and, where appropriate, access to resources (PFLAG and Mermaids in the UK offer support specifically for family members).

Not all family responses improve, and maintaining wellbeing sometimes means limiting contact with family members whose response is permanently harmful. Community — found family — is one of LGBTQ+ culture's most distinctive and valuable offerings to its members.

## Mental Health and Wellbeing

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-mentalhealth.jpg" alt="LGBTQ+ Mental Health"]

LGBTQ+ people experience significantly higher rates of depression, anxiety, self-harm and suicidal ideation than the general population. This is not an intrinsic feature of LGBTQ+ identity — it is a consequence of **minority stress**: the chronic stress produced by stigma, discrimination, concealment of identity, and the experience of living in environments that do not affirm who you are.

Understanding this is important in both directions. LGBTQ+ people benefit from knowing that their mental health challenges have an external cause — they are not evidence of something wrong with their identity, but of something wrong with the environment they have had to navigate. And the most effective mental health support for LGBTQ+ people is, accordingly, affirmative: it works with rather than against LGBTQ+ identity.

### LGBTQ+ Affirmative Therapy

LGBTQ+ affirmative therapy is practiced by clinicians who explicitly validate and affirm the client's sexual orientation and gender identity rather than treating it as a problem to be resolved. Conversion therapy — the practice of attempting to change sexual orientation or gender identity through any intervention — is harmful, ineffective, and now illegal in the UK for under-18s; legislation extending this to adults is in progress at time of writing.

Finding an affirmative therapist: the Pink Therapy directory lists LGBTQ+ affirmative therapists in the UK. MindOut is an LGBTQ+ specific mental health service. The NHS talking therapies service (IAPT) should always provide affirmative therapy.

### Specific Considerations by Group

**Trans and non-binary people** experience the highest mental health burden within the LGBTQ+ spectrum, compounded significantly by long NHS waiting times for gender identity services. Accessing peer support (Gendered Intelligence, Mermaids, Stonewall) and private or bridging healthcare where financially possible, and finding community among people who share the experience, are the most effective mitigations.

**Bisexual people** report poorer mental health than both straight and gay/lesbian people, linked strongly to bisexual erasure and the isolation of not feeling fully accepted in either straight or gay communities. Community specifically for bi people (BiUK, local bi groups) provides validation unavailable in broader LGBTQ+ spaces.

**LGBTQ+ people of colour** navigate the intersection of racial and LGBTQ+ minority stress, and may also face specific cultural or religious contexts in which LGBTQ+ identity is particularly contested. Organisations including Stonewall's People of Colour programme, Galop, and specific community groups offer targeted support.

**Older LGBTQ+ people** — who came out in, or lived through, more hostile periods of history — carry specific experiences and may have specific needs. Age UK's LGBTQ+ resources and Opening Doors London support older LGBTQ+ adults.

**LGBTQ+ people with disabilities** — intersecting needs that are often poorly served by both disability services and LGBTQ+ services. Specific support is increasingly available through online communities.

## Sexual Health: A Comprehensive Overview

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-sexualhealth.jpg" alt="LGBTQ+ Sexual Health"]

Sexual health for LGBTQ+ people is frequently poorly served by services designed around heterosexual, cisgender populations. Understanding your specific risks and options is the most effective form of protection.

### Testing

**How often:** every three months for people with multiple partners or higher-risk exposure (particularly gay and bisexual men); every six to twelve months for those in monogamous relationships or with lower exposure.

**What to test for:** a full STI screen includes HIV, syphilis, gonorrhoea, chlamydia, herpes (if symptomatic) and hepatitis B. Hepatitis C testing is relevant for gay and bisexual men who engage in rougher sex practices (fisting, toy sharing without barriers) where blood contact is possible.

**Where to test:** NHS sexual health clinics offer comprehensive testing. Free home test kits are available via SH:24 (shtwentyfour.org) and numerous NHS postcode-specific services. Many services offer LGBTQ+ specific clinics.

### Vaccination

**Hepatitis A and B:** recommended for all LGBTQ+ people; free on the NHS.
**HPV:** offered to all young people in the UK as part of the school immunisation programme (now to age 25 via catch-up); also available for gay and bisexual men up to age 45 via sexual health clinics.
**Mpox:** recommended for gay, bisexual and MSM with multiple partners; available via sexual health clinics.

### HIV

**PrEP** is available free on the NHS in England (via sexual health clinics), Scotland and Wales. It is taken daily or event-based (two pills before, one 24 hours after, one 48 hours after — only appropriate for planned sex, not reliable for spontaneous encounters). PrEP does not protect against other STIs; condoms remain the most effective barrier against the full range.

**U=U** (Undetectable = Untransmittable): a person with HIV who is on effective treatment and has an undetectable viral load cannot transmit HIV to their partners. This is established fact, not theory.

**PEP:** take within 72 hours of potential exposure; 28-day course. Available from sexual health clinics and A&E.

## Finding Community

[image src="media/img/lgbtq-community.jpg" alt="LGBTQ+ Community"]

Community is one of the most protective factors for LGBTQ+ mental health and wellbeing. Finding your people — in person, online, or both — is not peripheral to LGBTQ+ life; it is central to it.

**Pride events** — from local town Prides to massive city events — are the most visible community gathering points. They serve different functions for different people: celebration, protest, first visible community, reunion, simply fun.

**LGBTQ+ centres** exist in most major UK cities: London's Switchboard and the London LGBTQ+ Community Centre, Manchester's The LGBT Foundation, Glasgow's LGBT Centre, and many others offer services, events and community space.

**Online communities** are particularly valuable for people in areas with smaller physical communities, for those not yet out, and for people whose specific identity has a smaller local presence. Subreddits, Discord servers, and platforms like Tumblr host active, identity-specific communities for every point on the LGBTQ+ spectrum.

**Sports, social and hobby groups** — LGBTQ+ sports leagues, book clubs, choirs, gaming groups — offer community built around shared interests rather than identity alone, which many people find more comfortable as a point of entry.

**Chosen family** — deep, lasting bonds formed through community that function with the closeness and commitment of family — is one of the most distinctive and valuable aspects of LGBTQ+ culture. For people whose biological families are unsupportive or estranged, chosen family is not a consolation prize; it is a central source of belonging.

## A Final Word

Sexuality and gender are not problems to be solved. They are not phases to be grown out of, disorders to be treated, or sins to be overcome. They are fundamental, human, irreducible parts of who you are.

The LGBTQ+ spectrum exists because human beings are genuinely this varied — in desire, in gender, in the shapes their love and attraction take. Every point on that spectrum represents a real person living a real life, deserving of the same dignity, pleasure, safety, community and love as anyone else.

Wherever you are in understanding yourself — certain and settled, newly discovering, still questioning — you are not alone. The communities described in this guide exist because the people in them built them, often in the face of significant opposition, specifically so that whoever came after them would have somewhere to belong.

You do.
