## Safe Sex: A Practical Guide

[image src="media/img/Safe.jpg" alt="SafeSex"]

Safe sex is not a buzzkill. Done well, it is invisible — woven into the encounter without interrupting its momentum — and it is the foundation that allows sex to be enjoyed without anxiety. The goal of this guide is practical: what to use, when to use it, what to test for, and how to discuss it with a partner.

Nothing in this guide replaces medical advice from a clinician familiar with your personal health. Use it as orientation, then talk to a doctor or sexual health clinic for anything specific.

---

## Why It Matters

Two real risks: sexually transmitted infections (STIs) and unintended pregnancy. Both are common and both are largely preventable. The relative weight of each depends on the partners and the kind of sex, but the underlying principle is the same: a small amount of deliberate planning removes most of the risk and almost all of the worry.

A second, less obvious benefit: couples who discuss and practice safer sex tend to report higher levels of trust and intimacy, not lower. The conversation itself is a form of care.

## Barrier Methods

[image src="media/img/Safe1.jpg" alt="SafeSex"]

**External (male) condoms.** The most widely available and most-studied barrier method. Effective against most STIs and against pregnancy. Use a new condom for each act, including each shift between oral, vaginal, and anal contact. Check the expiry date, open the wrapper carefully (not with teeth), pinch the tip to leave space, and roll down to the base.

Use only **water-based or silicone-based lubricant** with latex condoms — oil-based lube (including coconut oil, baby oil, and many massage oils) degrades latex and causes failure. Polyurethane and polyisoprene condoms are alternatives for those with latex allergies.

**Internal (female) condoms.** A nitrile pouch inserted into the vagina or anus. Less commonly used but offer comparable protection and can be inserted in advance.

**Dental dams.** A thin sheet of latex or polyurethane used during oral-vaginal or oral-anal contact. A cut-open condom serves the same purpose if a dam is not available.

**Gloves and finger cots.** Useful for manual stimulation, particularly anal, and especially when fingernails or small cuts on the hands are a concern.

## Hormonal And Long-Acting Contraception

If pregnancy prevention is a goal and both partners have been recently tested, hormonal and long-acting methods offer high effectiveness rates and remove the need to think about contraception during sex.

- **Combined and progestin-only pills.** Highly effective when taken correctly. Daily routine required.
- **Implants and IUDs.** Among the most effective forms of contraception available — failure rates below 1% per year. Last three to ten years depending on the device.
- **Patches, rings, and injections.** Effective alternatives for those who prefer not to take a daily pill.
- **Vasectomy and tubal ligation.** Permanent options for those certain they do not want (further) children.

These methods do **not** protect against STIs. If STI risk is present, use a barrier method as well.

---

## Emergency Contraception

If a condom fails or contraception is missed, emergency contraception (the morning-after pill) is most effective when taken as soon as possible after unprotected sex — within 24 hours ideally, but useful up to 72 or 120 hours depending on the formulation. Available without prescription in most countries. The copper IUD, fitted within five days, is the most effective form of emergency contraception available.

## STI Testing

Regular testing is the single most useful sexual health habit, and the most consistently neglected. Many STIs are asymptomatic — particularly chlamydia, gonorrhoea, and HIV in early stages — which means feeling fine is not evidence of being clear.

Reasonable cadence:

- **Annual full screen** for anyone sexually active.
- **Every three to six months** for anyone with multiple partners or new partners.
- **Before unprotected sex with a new exclusive partner** — both partners test, share results, then decide together.
- **Any time symptoms appear:** unusual discharge, sores, persistent itching, painful urination, unexplained pelvic pain. Get tested, not because the worst is likely, but because most STIs are easily treated when caught early.

A standard full screen typically covers chlamydia, gonorrhoea, syphilis, HIV, and hepatitis B and C. Ask specifically about HPV and herpes — they are often not included by default.

## PrEP And Treatment As Prevention

For HIV specifically, two interventions have changed the landscape:

- **PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis).** A daily pill (or, increasingly, a long-acting injection) that, taken correctly, reduces the risk of acquiring HIV by more than 99%. Available through sexual health services in most countries. Worth discussing with a clinician for anyone whose risk profile includes condomless sex with new partners.

- **U=U (undetectable = untransmittable).** A person living with HIV who has an undetectable viral load on treatment cannot transmit HIV sexually. This is one of the most important findings of recent HIV research and has transformed how serodiscordant couples can think about their sex life.

## Talking About It

The conversation is the hardest part for most people. A few principles that make it easier:

- **Have it before things heat up,** not in the middle of taking clothes off.
- **Frame it as care, not interrogation.** *"When were you last tested? I'd like us both to be on the same page."* Not: *"Are you clean?"* (Which is a poor question — "clean" implies the alternative is "dirty," which is both stigmatising and inaccurate.)
- **Share first.** *"I tested two months ago, all clear, and I haven't been with anyone since. How about you?"* gives a partner something concrete to respond to.
- **Treat condoms as a default, not a negotiation.** Bringing them is care. Putting them on is part of the experience. A partner who tries to talk you out of using one is telling you something important about how they think about your wellbeing.

## Specific Situations

**New partner, no recent tests.** Use barriers for everything until both partners have tested.

**Long-term monogamous couple.** Once both have tested clear and agreed to monogamy, barrier use can be a choice rather than a necessity — but the agreement should be explicit, not assumed.

**Non-monogamous arrangements.** Establish, in advance and in writing if helpful, what barrier use is required with people outside the primary relationship, and what testing cadence is expected. Renegotiate as needed.

**Anal sex.** The rectal tissue is thinner and more prone to micro-tears than vaginal tissue, which raises the risk of STI transmission. Always use plenty of lube and a condom unless STI status is mutually confirmed. Never move from anal to vaginal contact without changing condom or thoroughly washing — UTI risk is significant.

**Oral sex.** Low but not zero STI risk. Barrier use (condoms, dental dams) is the gold standard with new partners; many people accept the lower risk in established relationships after testing.

---

## In Closing

Safe sex is a practice, not a single decision. The couples who do it best tend to share three habits:

1. They test regularly without it being a big deal.
2. They keep barriers and lube on hand so the decision to use them does not become a negotiation in the moment.
3. They talk about sexual health the way they talk about other shared logistics — practically, kindly, and without shame.

Done this way, safe sex disappears into the background and leaves the sex itself the only thing left to think about. Which is rather the point.
