## Squirting & Female Ejaculation: A Complete Guide

[image src="media/img/squirting-intro.jpg" alt="Squirting Guide"]

Squirting is one of the most discussed and least well-understood phenomena in sexual health. It has been mythologised, dismissed, exaggerated, and misrepresented in roughly equal measure. The result is that a great many people — both those who want to experience it and those who want to facilitate it in a partner — carry significant misinformation about what it actually is, where it comes from, and whether it is even possible for them.

The clear answer, supported by a substantial body of research, is that squirting is a genuine physiological phenomenon that is possible for most people with a vagina, given the right conditions and sufficient understanding of the underlying anatomy and technique. It is not a performance. It is not rare. And it is not, as is sometimes assumed, something that only happens to a specific type of person or in specific circumstances.

This guide covers the anatomy in detail, the distinction between squirting and female ejaculation, the psychological and physiological conditions required, the specific techniques that produce it, how to make it happen during sex, and what to do if it does not come easily at first.

## What Squirting Actually Is

Before discussing technique, it is worth establishing precisely what squirting is — because confusion about this is one of the primary reasons people approach it with anxiety rather than curiosity.

**Squirting** is the expulsion of a larger quantity of fluid from the urethra, typically during or just before orgasm, in response to stimulation of the anterior (front) wall of the vagina. Research has established that this fluid is primarily a diluted form of urine, produced and held in the bladder during sexual arousal, and expelled involuntarily as the arousal reaches a particular threshold. A 2022 study confirmed the bladder as the primary source. This is why many people feel a strong sensation of needing to urinate just before squirting — because, in a physiological sense, that sensation and squirting share the same neural pathway, even though the experience and the fluid composition are distinct from ordinary urination.

**Female ejaculation** is an entirely different process that is frequently confused with squirting. It involves the expulsion of a much smaller quantity of fluid — typically only a few millilitres — from the Skene's glands (sometimes called the female prostate), which sit at the lower end of the urethra. This fluid is chemically distinct from urine: it is thicker, whiter, and contains prostatic-specific antigen (PSA), the same compound found in male ejaculate. Researcher Beverly Whipple has described it as looking like watered-down skimmed milk, tasting sweet, and carrying no smell of urine.

In practice, both processes can occur simultaneously, and many people cannot tell them apart by sensation alone. What matters for the purposes of learning to squirt is understanding that the feeling of needing to urinate during intense G-spot stimulation is not a sign that something has gone wrong — it is a sign that you are on the right path. Suppressing that sensation, by clenching the pelvic floor or stopping stimulation, is the most common reason people fail to squirt despite being on the threshold of doing so.

## The Anatomy You Need To Understand

### The G Spot

The G spot — named for gynaecologist Ernst Gräfenberg, who first described it — is not a discrete anatomical structure in the way that, say, the clitoris is. It is better understood as a region of the anterior (front) vaginal wall that, when stimulated in an aroused person, produces intense sensation and, in many cases, squirting and ejaculation.

It is located approximately 5–7 centimetres (2–3 inches) inside the vaginal canal, along the front wall — the wall closest to the stomach rather than the spine. When unaroused, it can be difficult to locate or feel. When aroused, the tissue in this area swells and becomes noticeably textured — often described as feeling like a wet raspberry, a ridged or ribbed surface, or a slightly rough patch compared to the surrounding smooth tissue. This textural difference is the most reliable way to identify it.

To find it with fingers: insert one or two fingers with the palm facing upward (toward the stomach), curl them slightly, and slide along the front wall until you feel that change in texture. It will be there. In most people it is more obvious when they are already aroused and the tissue has had time to engorge.

### The Skene's Glands

The Skene's glands — sometimes called the female prostate — sit at the lower end of the urethra, within the tissue of the G-spot region. They are the source of female ejaculatory fluid, and they function in a directly analogous way to the male prostate. During sexual arousal, these glands fill with fluid, and during orgasm — particularly G-spot orgasm — they expel it.

The Skene's glands are not consistently developed to the same degree in all people. Research suggests that some people have more tissue here than others, which may partly explain why squirting comes very easily to some people and requires more work in others. However, the absence of abundant tissue does not mean squirting is impossible — it means the stimulation needs to be more precise and sustained.

### Why This Anatomy Matters

Understanding the anatomy removes a great deal of anxiety and confusion from the process. When you know that:

- the sensation of needing to urinate is expected and normal,
- the G spot swells during arousal and is easier to find when aroused,
- the Skene's glands need time to fill before they can be emptied,

you can approach squirting as a process with predictable, learnable steps rather than as an unpredictable event that either happens or does not.

## The Psychological Conditions: The Most Important Factor

[image src="media/img/squirting-relaxation.jpg" alt="Relaxation and Psychological Readiness"]

The single most common reason people fail to squirt despite technically competent stimulation is psychological — specifically, the combination of anxiety about urinating, performance pressure, and insufficient arousal.

### The Urination Anxiety Problem

Because the sensation immediately preceding squirting is nearly identical to the sensation of needing to urinate, the natural response for many people is to tense the pelvic floor and suppress it. This is exactly the opposite of what is required. Squirting requires the pelvic floor to release — to actively let go — at the moment when the sensation is most intense.

The most effective way to address this is practical: use the toilet before any session where you intend to try squirting. Knowing that the bladder is essentially empty removes the factual basis for the anxiety, even if it does not remove the sensation entirely. Many people find that after emptying the bladder, they can allow the familiar sensation to arise and then actively choose to release into it rather than suppress it.

Placing a towel or a waterproof sheet on the bed also helps — removing the practical concern about mess frees mental energy to focus on the sensation rather than the consequences.

### Arousal Threshold

Squirting is considerably harder to achieve from a state of partial arousal than from a state of full, sustained arousal. The Skene's glands need time to fill. The G-spot tissue needs time to engorge. And psychologically, a person who is only partially aroused will be more tense, less present, and less able to let go at the critical moment.

This means that any attempt to rush to G-spot stimulation without adequate foreplay is working against the very physiology that makes squirting possible. A thorough, unhurried arousal phase — one that brings the body to a state of genuine readiness — is not an optional prelude but a necessary part of the technique.

### Stress, Anxiety, and Inhibition

Any psychological state that activates the stress response will make squirting significantly harder. Stress, performance anxiety, relationship tension, self-consciousness about body image, and fear of the experience itself all work through the same mechanism: they activate the sympathetic nervous system, which is directly opposed to the parasympathetic state required for full sexual arousal.

If squirting does not happen on a first or second attempt, this is almost always a reflection of insufficient relaxation rather than a physiological inability. Creating the right psychological conditions — a private, unhurried setting, with a partner who is patient and attentive — matters as much as technique.

## Preparation

### For Partners: Fingernails

If G-spot stimulation will be provided by fingers, the nails on those fingers must be trimmed and filed smooth before any attempt. Trimming alone is insufficient — the edge left by clippers can still cause discomfort or injury to the vaginal tissue. Filing removes that edge completely.

The skin on the hands should also be reasonably soft. Rough or very dry skin on the fingertips creates friction against delicate tissue. Moisturiser applied a few hours before a session resolves this.

### The Towel

Place a towel or waterproof sheet on the bed. Knowing it is there removes one source of practical anxiety and allows both partners to focus entirely on the experience.

### Use The Toilet First

Empty the bladder beforehand. This does not prevent squirting — squirting draws on fluid that accumulates in the bladder during arousal — but it removes the factual basis for urination anxiety and makes it easier to release into the sensation rather than suppress it.

### Lubrication

The vaginal tissue, particularly during extended stimulation, benefits from additional lubrication. Natural arousal fluid is sufficient for many people, but supplementary lubricant — applied to the fingers or toy being used — reduces friction and allows more sustained stimulation without discomfort. Water-based lubricants are appropriate for use with silicone toys; silicone-based lubricants are longer-lasting and require less frequent reapplication.

## Fingering Techniques For Squirting

[image src="media/img/squirting-technique.jpg" alt="G Spot Stimulation Techniques"]

The following techniques are listed in order of intensity. Most people will respond to one of the first three; the Arm Shaker is a high-intensity technique designed for people who need more sustained pressure.

In all cases: begin with one finger, using the pad (the fingerprint side) against the front wall. Once you have located the G-spot region and the person is responding positively, introduce a second finger — typically the middle and ring fingers together — using the same orientation.

### 1. Push The Button

Using the fingertip(s) pressed against the G-spot, apply rhythmic pressure — pressing in and releasing, like pressing a button. Vary the pressure from light to firm and find the level that produces the most pronounced response.

This technique works well for initial exploration and for building arousal, but for many people it is not intense enough on its own to produce squirting. It is most useful as a starting point before progressing to more dynamic motion.

### 2. Circular Rubbing

Maintaining consistent pressure against the G-spot, move the fingertips in small, firm circles. This provides more continuous stimulation than the press-and-release of the button technique, and many people find it more pleasurable.

Again, this may be sufficient on its own for some people, and for others it functions best as part of a sequence.

### 3. The Come-Hither Motion

This is the technique most commonly associated with G-spot stimulation and squirting. With the fingers pressed against the G-spot, curl and uncurl them in a beckoning motion — the same gesture you would use to wave someone toward you. The movement should be firm and rhythmic, with pressure applied primarily as the fingers curl inward (pressing into the G-spot) and released slightly as they straighten.

The pace can vary: slower and more deliberate for building arousal, faster and more intense as the person approaches the threshold. For many people, this technique is sufficient to produce squirting without any further intervention.

### 4. The Arm Shaker

For people who need more sustained, intense pressure than the wrist and fingers alone can provide, the Arm Shaker extends the effort through the whole arm. The fingers remain in contact with the G-spot, curved slightly upward. Instead of using only the wrist and fingers to generate the motion, you stiffen the arm and use the entire limb to create a rapid up-and-down movement, applying considerably more force to the G-spot than fingers alone can sustain.

This technique requires more physical effort from the giver, but it is capable of producing the kind of sustained, intense stimulation that moves a person from the threshold of squirting across it. ### 5. Combined External Pressure

If G-spot stimulation alone is not producing squirting, applying gentle downward pressure to the mons pubis (the soft mound of tissue above the pubic bone) with the free hand can increase the sensation by gently squeezing the G-spot from the outside.

The pressure should be gentle — firm enough to create an additional sensation, light enough not to create discomfort or press significantly on the bladder. This technique can also be self-applied during partner stimulation, freeing the partner's hand for other uses.

### 6. Combined G-Spot And Clitoral Stimulation

For many people, squirting is most easily achieved when G-spot stimulation is combined with clitoral stimulation. The two nerve pathways, when activated simultaneously, create a level of arousal that makes it significantly easier to cross the threshold.

This can be done by one partner using both hands — one inside, one on the clitoris — or by the receiving partner stimulating their own clitoris while the other provides G-spot stimulation. The latter approach also allows the receiving partner to control the intensity of clitoral stimulation independently, which is useful because for many people the ideal clitoral intensity is different from what a partner would intuitively provide.

## The Moment Before Squirting: What To Do

[image src="media/img/squirting-release.jpg" alt="Releasing Into Squirting"]

The moment immediately preceding squirting is the one where most people instinctively work against the process rather than with it. The sensation of intense pressure and the urge to urinate arises, and the reflex is to clench and suppress.

The technique required at this moment is the opposite: actively relax the pelvic floor, push outward (as if about to urinate), and continue breathing. This deliberate release is what allows the fluid to be expelled rather than held back.

It is worth practising pelvic floor relaxation independently — simply learning what it feels like to deliberately release the muscle that is usually contracted during urination. During sex or masturbation, bringing that same conscious release to the moment of peak G-spot stimulation is what typically converts the threshold sensation into actual squirting.

The first time this succeeds, it often produces a relatively small amount of fluid. With practice and increasing familiarity with the sensation and the required response, the intensity and volume typically increase.

## Making Yourself Squirt Solo

[image src="media/img/squirting-solo.jpg" alt="Solo Squirting Techniques"]

Learning to squirt independently, before or alongside partner-based attempts, has several significant advantages. It removes the performance element entirely — there is no one else present to consider, no dynamic to manage, no anxiety about a partner's reactions. It also means you can take as much time as you need, adjust everything in real time based purely on your own sensations, and develop a precise understanding of exactly what kind of stimulation works for your particular body before trying to communicate that to a partner.

The anatomical principles and techniques are identical to those described above. The main practical difference is the positioning, since you are working with your own hands rather than a partner's.

### Useful Positions For Solo Squirting

**Lying on your back with knees bent** — the most natural starting position. Insert one or two fingers with the palm facing upward and locate the G-spot. The main limitation is wrist fatigue during sustained stimulation.

**Sitting or kneeling** — reduces the angle of the wrist and can make sustained stimulation more comfortable.

**All fours with one hand reaching forward** — allows gravity to shift slightly, which some people find helpful.

Wrist fatigue is the most common practical obstacle during solo sessions. Varying the technique — moving between press-and-release, come-hither, and arm-shaker approaches — extends the session and keeps the stimulation varied.

Vibrators and dildos specifically designed for G-spot stimulation are extremely useful for solo squirting, primarily because they resolve the wrist fatigue problem and can provide more consistent, sustained stimulation than fingers alone.

The most effective toys for this purpose have a pronounced upward curve at the head, designed to make consistent G-spot contact easy to maintain throughout a session. Rigid materials — hard plastic, glass, metal — tend to be more effective for G-spot stimulation because they transmit pressure more directly; softer silicone toys absorb some of the pressure and may not provide sufficient intensity for people who need more sustained force.

Vibrating G-spot toys add an additional stimulus that many people find significantly increases their ability to squirt, particularly if they have difficulty reaching the threshold with pressure alone. The vibration stimulates a different set of nerve endings simultaneously, adding to the overall level of arousal.

A vibrator used externally on the clitoris while a curved dildo or finger provides internal G-spot stimulation is one of the most consistently effective combinations for solo squirting.

## What To Do If Squirting Is Not Happening

[image src="media/img/squirting-troubleshooting.jpg" alt="Troubleshooting Squirting"]

If you have tried the techniques above and squirting has not occurred, the issue is almost certainly in one or more of the following areas rather than in any innate physiological limitation.

**Insufficient arousal.** The Skene's glands fill slowly during arousal. If stimulation of the G-spot begins before a person is fully aroused, the glands will not yet contain enough fluid to expel, and the G-spot tissue will not be sufficiently engorged to be maximally sensitive. The solution is more time — more foreplay, more attention to whole-body arousal before any G-spot stimulation begins.

**Suppression at the threshold.** If you are reaching an intense sensation and then clenching — physically holding yourself back from the feeling of releasing — this is the most likely cause of the block. The deliberate pelvic floor release described above is the specific response that converts this threshold sensation into squirting. Practise the release in isolation, and then bring it consciously to the moment when the sensation is strongest.

**Incorrect location.** It is possible to be stimulating a part of the front wall that is near but not exactly at the G-spot. The G-spot can be found by its texture, but it also tends to be identifiable by the specific quality of sensation it produces when stimulated — more intense, more urgent, often producing the sensation of needing to urinate more strongly than surrounding tissue. Methodical exploration with a finger, with explicit verbal feedback from the receiving partner, is the most reliable way to confirm that the correct location has been found.

**Technique not suited to this individual.** Different people respond to different levels of pressure and different types of motion. If the come-hither technique is not producing results, the arm shaker is worth attempting. If pressure stimulation alone is insufficient, combining it with clitoral stimulation may be what is needed. There is no single technique that works for everyone, and finding the right combination requires experimentation.

**Inconsistent or interrupted stimulation.** Squirting typically requires sustained, consistent stimulation over a period of time — not stimulation that stops and starts or frequently changes technique. Once a technique is producing a clear response, maintain it consistently and allow the sensation to build rather than switching to something else.

**Anxiety or performance pressure.** If the explicit goal of a session is to produce squirting, the pressure of that goal can itself be the primary obstacle. Some people find it helpful to take the goal off the table entirely for a period — focusing on pleasure rather than outcome — and find that squirting happens more readily in this lower-pressure context.

## Squirting FAQ

**Is squirting the same as urinating?**

Not exactly, but they are physiologically related. Squirting fluid is primarily a diluted form of urine — produced and held in the bladder during arousal — but its composition differs from ordinary urine due to both the dilution and the presence of small amounts of prostatic fluid from the Skene's glands. The experience is also entirely different from urination: it is involuntary, occurs during peak arousal, and is accompanied by intense sensation rather than relief.

**Can everyone squirt?**

The current evidence suggests that the capacity for squirting exists in most people with a vagina, since all have the relevant anatomy (Skene's glands, bladder, G-spot region). However, individual variation in the development of the Skene's glands means that some people produce very small amounts of ejaculatory fluid, and the volume of squirting varies significantly between individuals. For some people squirting comes very easily; for others it requires sustained practice and very specific conditions.

**Does squirting always accompany orgasm?**

No. Squirting and orgasm are related but separate events. Many people squirt at the moment of orgasm; others squirt before orgasm; others squirt without orgasming at all. The two events share a physiological proximity but are not the same thing.

**Is squirting supposed to feel pleasurable?**

Yes, for most people, the process of squirting is intensely pleasurable — both the build-up of pressure and the release itself. The sensation is often described as an intense, spreading warmth that is qualitatively different from clitoral orgasm. Some people find the experience so intense that it takes time to become comfortable with it; others find it immediately pleasurable. There is no correct response.

**Does the amount of fluid matter?**

No. The volume expelled varies enormously between individuals and between sessions — from a few drops to a much larger quantity. Neither extreme indicates anything about the quality of the experience or the degree of arousal. A small amount of fluid during a highly aroused, intensely pleasurable session is just as valid as a large amount.

**What if I feel like I need to urinate during stimulation but nothing comes out?**

This is extremely common and usually means one of two things: either the pelvic floor is being clenched in response to the sensation, suppressing the release; or the stimulation has not yet been sustained long enough for the fluid to be fully expelled. Actively relaxing the pelvic floor and pushing outward while maintaining stimulation will usually resolve this.

**How long does it take?**

It varies considerably. Some people squirt the first time they deliberately attempt it. For others, it takes multiple sessions over days or weeks. The most important variable is not the time spent trying but the degree of relaxation, arousal, and familiarity with the specific sensation — all of which develop with practice.

## A Final Note On Expectations

Squirting is not something that must happen for sex to be satisfying, successful, or intimate. It is one specific aspect of the vast range of experiences that sexual pleasure encompasses, and treating it as a goal to achieve or a performance to deliver places pressure on an experience that works best without pressure.

The value of learning the anatomy and techniques is not to guarantee a specific outcome but to remove the primary obstacles — misinformation, anxiety, and ineffective technique — that prevent many people from experiencing something that their body is, in most cases, entirely capable of. What happens after that is determined by the particular chemistry of a particular body, in a particular moment, with the particular stimulation that happens to work for that individual.

Curiosity, patience, and genuine attention to what produces pleasure — rather than anxiety about whether a specific thing happens — are the conditions under which squirting, like most forms of intense sexual experience, is most likely to occur.
